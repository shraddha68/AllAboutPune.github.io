Historical places in Pune!

Sinhagad Fort

Sinhagad, also known as Kondhana, is an ancient hill fortress located approximately 49 kilometers southwest of Pune, India. It has a rich history and has witnessed many battles, most notably the Battle of Sinhagad in 1670. The name “Sinhagad” translates to “Lion’s Fort”, signifying its strength and brilliance. Here are some key details about Sinhagad:

Historical Significance


Some evidence suggests that the fort could have been built around 2000 years ago. Caves and carvings in the Kaundinyeshwar temple support this theory. The fort was captured by forces of Muhammad bin Tughluq in 1328 after a siege of eight months. It then became part of the Bahmani Sultanate and later the Nizam Shahi of Ahmadnagar. Shahaji Bhosale, commander of Ibrahim Adil Shah II, gained control of Kondana in 1647.

Shaniwar Wada

Shaniwar Wada, located in Pune, India, is a historical fortification that played a significant role in Maratha history. Here are some fascinating details about this remarkable place:

Historical Significance


Built in 1732, Shaniwar Wada served as the great seat of the Peshwas (prime ministers) of the Maratha Empire until 1818. During the 18th century, it became the center of Indian politics due to the rise of the Maratha Empire. The fort itself was largely destroyed in 1828 by an unexplained fire, but the surviving structures are now maintained as a tourist site. The imposing walls, strong teak gateways, and deep foundations still stand as a testament to its historical significance. Legend has it that the ghost of Peshwa Narayanrao, who was murdered in Shaniwar Wada, calls for help on full moon nights. Visitors and locals have allegedly heard the cries of “Kaka mala vachava” (Uncle, save me) by Narayanrao Peshwa after his death.

Construction


Peshwa Baji Rao I, prime minister to Chattrapati Shahu, laid the ceremonial foundation of his own residence on Saturday, 10 January 1730. Although the top six stories collapsed after being attacked by British artillery, the stone base of Shaniwar Wada remains and can be seen even today in the older parts of Pune.

Lal Mahal

Lal Mahal, also known as the Red Palace, is one of the most famous monuments in Pune, India. It holds significant historical importance and is closely associated with Shivaji I, the founder of the Maratha Empire. Let’s delve into the intriguing history and features of this remarkable place:

Historical Significance


In the year 1630 AD, Shivaji’s father, Shahaji Raje Bhosale, established the Lal Mahal for his wife Jijabai and their son, Shivaji. Shivaji spent several years in the Lal Mahal until he captured his first fort. The original Lal Mahal was built with the intention of rejuvenating the recently razed city of Pune when Shahaji Raje entered the city along with Shivaji and his mother, Maasaheb Jijabai. Young Shivaji grew up within the walls of the Lal Mahal, residing there until he conquered the Torna fort in 1645. Notably, Shivaji’s marriage to his first wife, Saibai, took place in the Lal Mahal on 16 May 1640.

Decline and Reconstruction


Towards the end of the 17th century, the original Lal Mahal fell into ruin due to various attacks on the city. During the construction of the Shaniwarwada, some soil and stones from the Lal Mahal were used for luck. The current Lal Mahal, a reconstruction, stands in the center of Pune. It was built by the Pune Municipal Corporation between 1984 and 1988. The memorial now houses a collection of large-size oil paintings depicting significant events in Shivaji’s life, a statue of Jijabai, a carving showing Shivaji using a gold plow alongside Jijabai, a fiber model of Raigad with horsemen, and a huge map of Maharashtra indicating Shivaji’s forts.

Aga Khan Palace

Construction and Purpose:

The Aga Khan Palace was built by Sultan Muhammed Shah Aga Khan III in 1892. It stands as an act of charity by the spiritual leader of the Nizari Ismaili Muslims. The Sultan’s intention was to help the poor in the neighboring areas of Pune, who were severely affected by famine. He provided them with employment during the construction of the palace. The construction involved hundreds of thousands of people and cost 1.2 million rupees.

Historical Significance

The Aga Khan Palace played a crucial role in the Indian freedom movement. During the Quit India Movement, it served as a prison for several prominent leaders, including Mahatma Gandhi, his wife Kasturba Gandhi, and his secretary Mahadev Desai. Sarojini Naidu and others were also imprisoned here, demanding an end to British rule in India. Tragically, both Kasturba Gandhi and Mahadev Desai passed away while in captivity at the palace.

Memorials and Recognition

The Aga Khan Palace is closely linked to Mahatma Gandhi: His ashes were kept here, and the palace now houses a memorial dedicated to him. Kasturba Gandhi and Mahadev Desai also have their Samadhis (memorial shrines) within the same complex, near the Mula river. In 2003, the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) declared the site a monument of national importance.

Current State

The palace covers an area of 7.7 hectares (19 acres), with the built-up portion spanning 2.8 hectares (6.9 acres). It was once home to a residential co-education school from 1953 to 1972. In 1969, Aga Khan IV donated the palace to the Indian people as a mark of respect for Gandhi and his philosophy. Today, visitors can explore the palace, learn about its history, and pay homage to the great leaders who were part of its story.

Vishrambaugwada

Vishrambaug Wada, situated on Bajirao Road in Pune, Maharashtra, India, is a three-storeyed mansion with a fascinating history. Let’s delve into its intriguing details:

Historical Context

Built in 1807 AD by Peshwa Baji Rao II, Vishrambaug Wada served as the luxurious residence of the last Peshwa of the Maratha confederacy. Unlike the ill-fated Shaniwar Wada, Baji Rao II preferred this mansion as his abode. The construction took six years to complete, and it cost Rs 200,000. Vishrambaug Wada stands as a testament to Pune’s rich culture and heritage. Baji Rao II resided here for eleven years until his defeat in the Third Anglo-Maratha War. After the war, the British exiled him to Bithur near Kanpur, while his wife, Varanasibai, continued to stay at Vishrambaug Wada for a while. In 1821, the East India Company started the Hindoo college within the wada to promote Sanskrit learning in Pune. This college eventually evolved into the Poona College, which later became Deccan College. The wada also housed the Vishrambag High School.

Architectural Features

The wada boasts a fine entrance and a balcony adorned with intricate woodwork. Its dimensions are impressive: 260 feet by 815 feet. The mansion symbolizes the tales of Pune’s glorious past and its connection to the Peshwa dynasty.

Transformation and Restoration

Over time, the wada served various purposes, including housing the Municipality and its successor, the Pune Municipal Corporation (PMC). In 1871, an act of arson destroyed the eastern wing of the wada. In 1930, the PMC purchased the property from the colonial Bombay Presidency government. Restoration efforts are ongoing under the Heritage Corridor Plan, with special attention to preserving the original bricks, windows, wooden pillars, and motifs. The red-tiled sloping roof and the courtyard, typical of Maratha architecture, will be retained.

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Bridge

The Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Bridge, also known as Nava Pul, is celebrating its centenary. Constructed in 1923, this bridge stands as a testament to both aesthetics and engineering marvel. It has withstood the test of time, including the 1961 Panshet floods. The bridge connects Bhamburda to old Pune and is considered a Grade-II heritage structure. Originally known as the Lloyd Bridge, it was built by Raobahadur Ganpatrao Mahadeo Kenjale during the British Rule under the aegis of Sir George Lloyd, the then governor of Bombay Presidency. After India won independence, it was renamed the Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Bridge. This iconic bridge begins at Mangala Theatre and alights in front of Shaniwar Wada. Made with black basalt, it not only stands as a testament to strong quality work but also boasts delicate carvings on both sides. The bridge serves as a landmark connecting PMC with the historic Shaniwar Wada and overseeing Pune’s Public Transport System, PMPML’s hub. It spans the Mutha River and has been given Grade-II heritage status by PMC. A special celebration is being organized by the Kenjale family on September 17 at 3 pm on the Shaniwar Wada side of the bridge. The event is open to all and will include the unveiling of a special documentary made on the Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Bridge. During the commemorative ceremony, the cornerstone marking the completion of 100 years of this historic bridge will be laid by Chandrakant Patil, the Guardian Minister of Pune. The bridge will be adorned with flowers and traditional ‘rangoli’, and the customary ‘dhol-taasha’ will add to the festivities. This bridge, which initially linked Bhamburda to Pune, stands as a testament to the city’s growth and transformation over the past century. On September 16, 1923, the Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Bridge, known as the Victoria Bridge at the time, was inaugurated.

Shivneri Fort

Shivneri Fort, located near Junnar in Pune district, Maharashtra, India, holds historical significance as the birthplace of Chatrapati Shivaji Maharaj, the visionary founder of the Maratha Empire.

History

Shivneri Fort was initially under the possession of the Yadavas of Devagiri. It played a crucial role in guarding the old trading route from Desh to the port city of Kalyan. Over time, it passed through various dynasties, including the Bahmani Sultanate and the Ahmadnagar Sultanate. In 1595, Maloji Bhosale, the grandfather of Shivaji Bhosale, was granted control of Shivneri by the Ahmadnagar Sultan, Bahadur Nizam Shah. Shivaji himself was born at this fort on February 19, 1630 (some accounts place it in 1627) and spent his childhood there. Inside the fort, you’ll find a small temple dedicated to goddess Shivai Devi, after whom Shivaji was named.

Architecture

Shivneri Fort stands atop a hill, shaped like a triangle, with its entrance on the southwest side. The main gate and a secondary entrance (locally known as the “chain gate”) provide access. The fort boasts seven well-defended gates along its 1-mile (1.6 km) stretch. Mud walls surround the entire fort. Notable structures within include a prayer hall, a tomb, a mosque, and an overhanging area where executions took place. The central water pond, called “Badami Talav,” adds to the fort’s charm. Statues of Jijabai and a young Shiva grace the premises. Additionally, two perennial water springs named Ganga and Yamuna are found within the fort. Just 2 kilometers away lies the Buddhist rock-cut caves known as Lenyadri caves, which also house one of the Ashtavinayak temples in Maharashtra1.

UNESCO Recognition:

In 2021, Shivneri Fort was added to the tentative list of UNESCO World Heritage sites as part of the “Serial Nomination of Maratha Military Architecture in Maharashtra”.

Shinde Chhatri

Shinde Chhatri, located in Wanawadi, Pune, India, is a memorial dedicated to the 18th-century Maratha military leader Mahadji Shinde. Mahadji Shinde served as the commander-in-chief of the Maratha army under the Peshwas from 1760 to 1780. This significant landmark in the city commemorates the Maratha rule and holds historical importance. The three-storied memorial, built in Rajput architectural style, marks the spot of Mahadji Shinde’s cremation on February 12, 1794. Originally, the complex only had a temple dedicated to Lord Shiva, which Mahadji Shinde himself constructed. His last rites were performed in the temple premises. Later, in 1910, a samadhi (memorial) was added outside the sanctum of the Shiva temple, precisely where he was cremated. Maharaja Madho Rao Scindia of Gwalior commissioned the construction of this complex, along with the memorial for Mahadji Shinde. The architectural grandeur of Shinde Chhatri is remarkable, reflecting the style used in Rajasthan, India. The building adheres to Vastu shastra rules and features exquisite carvings. The steeple of the Shiva temple showcases idols of saints made of yellow stone, while the base and sanctum sanctorum are constructed in black stone. The hall itself houses a gallery and boasts English-style colored window panes. Paintings and photographs of Shinde family members adorn the hall, preserving its architectural beauty and design1. Despite years of neglect, the memorial is now being restored to its original appearance, ensuring that its historical significance endures for generations to come.

Bhide Wada

Construction and Background:

Bhide Wada was constructed by the Bhide family in a thriving business district of Budhwar Peth during the rule of the Peshwas in Pune, before their eventual defeat in 1818. These wada-like structures were common across the city in the 18th and 19th centuries, resembling traditional cottages.

The Phule Connection:

In 1848, social reformers Jyotirao and Savitribai Phule established the first indigenous girls’ school of modern India within Bhide Wada. Despite facing opposition from traditionalists, Savitribai played a pivotal role in changing the course of women’s educational activism in modern India. Bhide Wada became a center of public activism, attracting prominent figures like Mahadev Govind Ranade and Dayanand Saraswati. Even after the school’s closure, Bhide Wada continued to be significant, shaping new political and cultural expressions in modern India.

Demolition and Redevelopment

Demolition and Redevelopment: On December 5, 2023, the Pune Municipal Corporation (PMC) demolished the old and dilapidated Bhide Wada. However, recognizing its historical importance, the state government plans to redevelop it into a national memorial to honor the Phule couple’s contribution to modern India.

Rasalgad

Rasalgad is a small hill fort located in the Sahyadri mountain range of Maharashtra, India. It stands at an impressive elevation of 1,770 feet (500 meters) above sea level . The fort’s highest point is crowned with a distinctive pinnacle that connects to nearby forts such as Suamargad and Mahipatgad . Rasalgad is known for its picturesque beauty, especially during the monsoon season. In 2003, it was officially recognized as a protected monument by the Indian Archaeological Survey . The fort offers breathtaking views of the surrounding Sahyadri hills and lush greenery. Its unique vantage point provides a mesmerizing perspective for visitors. If you’re ever in the region, consider exploring Rasalgad and immersing yourself in its historical and natural charm.

Tribal Museum

The Pune Tribal Museum, located in the city of Pune, Maharashtra, India, is a fascinating repository that celebrates the rich culture and lifestyle of the Maharashtrian tribes. Let me share some details about this unique museum:

Establishment and Purpose:

The museum was established in 1962 as an extension of the Tribal Research and Training Institute, Pune. It officially opened for public display in 1965. Its mission is to preserve and showcase the artistic and poetic impulses of tribals.

Exhibits:

The museum houses a diverse collection of artifacts representing the tribal communities of Maharashtra. Visitors can explore items such as:
Musical instruments, Jewellery, Tools and handicrafts, Costumes, Idols, Household articles, Photographs, Weapons, Warli paintings, Bamboo works, Masks, Utensils,
These exhibits provide insights into the lives, traditions, and creativity of 47 tribes from the region.

Location and Administration:

The museum is situated at 8 Queens Garden, Richardson Road, near the Railway Station, Pune, Maharashtra 411001. The Tribal Research and Training Institute currently oversees the administration of the museum.

Future Plans:

There are exciting plans to offer virtual tours of the museum to passengers waiting at Pune airport. This initiative aims to make the museum’s cultural treasures accessible to a wider audience.
